## The Decide and Adjust Method: How to Make Faster Decisions Without Regret

What if you could make most decisions in seconds, with less regret, and more confidence? The secret isn't thinking longer—it's learning to decide and adjust instead of deciding and dwelling.

Here's a truth nobody talks about: most decisions can be changed. The restaurant you chose? You can leave. The movie you picked? You can stop watching. The cereal you bought? You can eat something else tomorrow. The vast majority of "decisions" we agonize over are completely reversible. And once you internalize this truth, your entire relationship with decision-making transforms.

The "decide and adjust" method is simple: make the best choice you can with the information you have, commit to it, and be ready to adjust if needed. Instead of trying to make perfect decisions, you make good decisions and refine along the way. Here's how it works.

## The Core Philosophy: Decisions Are Editable

We treat decisions like they're cast in stone. We imagine that whatever we choose is permanent, irreversible, fate-changing. But for 90% of daily decisions, this simply isn't true. You can change restaurants after ordering. You can switch the music if your first choice doesn't work. You can return the shirt that looked better online. You can switch the chore assignment if it becomes clear it won't work.

The problem is that we treat every decision like it matters as much as choosing a career or a life partner. We use the same mental energy for "which restaurant should we try?" as we do for "which mortgage should I sign?" No wonder we're exhausted.

### The Adjust Part Is Crucial

Most advice says "make a decision and stick with it." This is good advice for major decisions but terrible advice for minor ones. The decide-and-adjust method adds a crucial element: the willingness to change course if the decision doesn't work out.

This isn't waffling. This isn't being indecisive. This is strategic flexibility. You make the best choice you can. You commit to it. But if new information shows you made the wrong call, you adjust. Quick course corrections beat slow, painful stuck-ness.

### The Decide and Adjust Framework
1
**Decide quickly:** Set a time limit. Make the best choice you can within that window. Don't seek perfection—seek "good enough to move forward."
2
**Commit fully:** Once decided, stop second-guessing. Give your choice a fair shot. Mental energy spent second-guessing is wasted energy.
3
**Watch for signals:** Pay attention to how the decision is playing out. Is this working? Is this comfortable? Is this fun? Feedback is data.
4
**Adjust if needed:** If the decision clearly isn't working, change course. Don't cling to a bad choice just because you already made it.

## Why This Reduces Regret

Here's something counterintuitive: the decide-and-adjust method actually reduces decision regret. Why? Because regret usually comes from two sources: (1) making a bad choice and suffering, and (2) not making a choice and wondering "what if?"

The decide-and-adjust method reduces both. By making quick decisions with the willingness to adjust, you reduce suffering (because you're monitoring and course-correcting). And you reduce "what if" thinking (because you actually made the choice—you didn't stay paralyzed).
> "A decision made and adjusted is worth more than a perfect decision that never got made."

## Practical Applications for Families

### Dinner Decisions

Instead of spending 20 minutes debating restaurants, use the decide-and-adjust method: pick something in 10 seconds, go there, and if it's terrible, leave and try somewhere else next time. The "adjust" part removes the pressure of making the "perfect" choice because you know you have a backup plan.

### Weekend Activities

Pick an activity for the morning. Commit to it fully. If it's not working out, adjust in the afternoon. Don't spend Friday night agonizing over Saturday's plan—make a quick choice and refine as you go.

### Kids' Decisions

Teach your kids this method: "Pick something. Give it a try. If it's not good, we can change." This reduces the paralysis that comes from feeling like each choice is permanent and high-stakes.

### Household Decisions

Arrange the furniture? Try it one way for a week. If it doesn't work, change it. Buy a new organizational system? Test it for a month. If it's not helping, return it. Most home decisions are easily reversible—the only thing you lose is the time you spent deliberating instead of experimenting.

## When to Use the Full Method vs. Quick Decisions

Not every decision needs the full decide-and-adjust treatment. Here's a quick guide:
- **Use full method for:** Decisions with multiple options, decisions that will be lived with for a while, decisions that could have significant consequences (even if small stakes).
- **Use quick decisions for:** Truly trivial choices, binary options, anything where perfection doesn't matter.
- **Use randomness for:** When time is more valuable than optimization. Let a coin flip or spin wheel decide, then move on completely.

## The Speed-Accuracy Tradeoff

Here's a truth that might change how you see decision-making: speed and accuracy are often inversely related in daily life. The longer you deliberate, the better your decision might be—but the time you spend deliberating is rarely worth the marginal improvement in the decision's quality.

Example: You spend 30 minutes choosing a restaurant. You pick one that might be slightly better than if you'd spent 5 minutes choosing. But you've now spent 25 extra minutes deliberating, which you'll never get back, for a restaurant meal that you'll forget about in a week. Is that worth it?

The decide-and-adjust method inverts the tradeoff. You save time by deciding fast. You maintain quality by being willing to adjust. And you reclaim hours that were previously spent in analysis paralysis.

## Teaching Kids to Decide and Adjust

The decide-and-adjust method is perfect for teaching kids because it makes decision-making feel safe. They can try things, adjust, try again—without pressure of permanent consequences. Here's how to introduce it:
1. "Pick something to play with."
2. "Great! Now give it a try."
3. "Not feeling it? No problem—pick something else."
4. Repeat. The lesson: decisions are experiments, not verdicts.

This works for food choices, activity choices, friend choices, hobby choices. When kids learn that decisions can be changed, they become more willing to make them. And more willing to learn from them.

## Your First Practice Session

Tomorrow, try this: pick one decision you'll face and apply the decide-and-adjust method. It could be dinner, an activity, a purchase—anything. Make the choice quickly (set a 10-second timer if you need to). Commit to it. Notice your urge to second-guess. Don't act on it. If the decision works, great. If it doesn't, adjust.

Do this three times in one day. By the end, you'll have proven to yourself that most decisions can be made fast, lived with, and adjusted if needed. That proof changes everything.

The goal isn't to stop caring about decisions. It's to care about them differently—less about making the perfect choice, more about making choices and making them work. Decide and adjust. That's the whole method.
