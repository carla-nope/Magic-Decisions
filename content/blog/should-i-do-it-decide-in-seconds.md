# Should I Do It? Decide in Seconds

It's 9 PM. You've been thinking about texting them all day. The message is right there, typed out, sitting in your drafts. Your thumb is hovering. Should you hit send or not?

"I've been working on this project for weeks. Should I just quit?" 

"My friend invited me somewhere but I'm tired. Should I go or stay home?"

"Should I apply for that job? The listing closes tomorrow..."

We've all been there. Those moments where you know you need to decide, but every time you try to think it through, you end up back where you started. The question loops, the anxiety grows, and somehow an hour has passed and you're still staring at the same screen.

The good news: there's a way out. And it's faster than you think.

## Why "Should I Do It" Questions Feel So Hard

Here's the thing about should I do it decisions: they're rarely actually hard. They're hard because we make them hard.

### The Overthinking Trap

When you ask yourself "should I do this?" your brain immediately goes into analysis mode. It starts building pros and cons lists. It imagines scenarios. It predicts outcomes. It tries to calculate whether this choice will lead to happiness or disaster.

But here's the problem: your brain is terrible at predicting the future. It can tell you what might happen, but it can't tell you what will happen. So all that analysis is just... spinning wheels. You're trying to solve an unsolvable equation.

The result? Analysis paralysis. The more you think, the less clear you become. The less clear you become, the more you think. It's a loop with no exit.

### The Fear Disguised as Logic

Most "should I do it" decisions aren't actually about the thing itself. They're about fear. Fear of rejection. Fear of failure. Fear of judgment. Fear of missing out. Fear of making the wrong choice.

But fear doesn't feel like fear. It feels like "I need more information." It feels like "I just need to think a little more." It feels like "I should be strategic about this."

So you analyze. And analyze. And the analysis gives you permission to not decide, which gives you temporary relief from the fear. But the fear comes back, because the question never got answered.

### The Identity Trap

Sometimes "should I do it" questions are hard because they're identity questions in disguise. "Should I apply for that job?" is really "Am I the kind of person who takes risks?" "Should I text them?" is really "Am I the kind of person who goes after what they want?"

When a decision becomes about who you are, the stakes feel infinite. You can always postpone a choice about what to do, but you can't postpone a choice about who you are. So you freeze.

## The Second Decision Method

Here's a simple technique that works surprisingly well: the second decision method.

When you're stuck on "should I do it," don't ask yourself what you should do. Instead, ask: "If I do X, and I hate it, can I reverse it in 5 seconds?"

Most of the time, you can.

- Text them and regret it? You can apologize or play it off.
- Go out and wish you'd stayed home? You can come back early.
- Apply for the job and not get it? Nothing happens except you tried.
- Wear the bold outfit and feel weird? Change clothes.

The second decision is always available. This means the first decision has less risk than it appears.

### The 10-Second Rule

Here's another angle: most "should I do it" decisions involve action. The action takes about 10 seconds. The thinking about the action has taken 3 hours.

10 seconds of action versus 3 hours of anxiety. The math doesn't make sense.

So next time you're stuck, set a timer. Count to 10. If you haven't decided by then, use the decision tool. Either way, stop the loop. Make a choice and move on.

## When You Actually Can't Decide

Sometimes the question really is hard. Sometimes there are real stakes. How do you know when "should I do it" deserves real thought versus quick resolution?

### Use This Checklist

Ask yourself these questions:

1. **What's the worst that happens?** If the worst is "I feel awkward for 5 minutes," that's a fast decision. If the worst is "I lose thousands of dollars or damage a relationship," that's worth more thought.

2. **Can I reverse this?** If yes, decide fast. If no, slow down.

3. **Is the fear of not doing it bigger than the fear of doing it?** Often it is. If you've been thinking about this for days and the thought of not doing it makes you sad, that's information.

4. **Am I looking for permission?** Sometimes you already know what you want. You're just looking for someone to give you the green light. If that's you, skip the analysis and decide.

### When to Think, When to Act

**Fast decisions (use a tool or decide now):**
- Social choices (text, call, invite, say hi)
- Low-stakes activities (go out, stay home, try something new)
- Minor purchases (buy the coffee, get the small upgrade, grab that thing)
- Communication choices (reply now, send the email, share that thing)

**Slow decisions (think and maybe wait):**
- Career changes
- Major financial decisions
- Relationship significant steps
- Anything involving other people's wellbeing

## The Quick Decision Maker Approach

When you genuinely can't figure out what to do, here's a structured approach to fast decision-making:

### Step 1: Name the Fear
What are you actually afraid of? Write it down. "I'm afraid they'll think I'm weird." "I'm afraid I'll fail." "I'm afraid I'll regret it."

Naming the fear takes away its power. It's no longer this vague sense of dread—it's a specific thing you can address.

### Step 2: Ask the Magic Question
"What would I do if I knew I'd succeed?" 

Most people who are stuck have a pretty good idea of what they should do. They're just scared of the outcome. The magic question bypasses the fear and asks: what does success look like? Usually, the answer is "do the thing I want to do."

### Step 3: Use a Decision Tool
If you're still stuck, use a quick decision maker app to externalize the choice. Don't think about whether the tool is "right"—just use it to break the loop. The goal isn't to find the perfect answer. The goal is to stop the anxiety spiral.

### Step 4: Take One Small Step
After deciding, don't plan the whole thing. Just take one step. Send the text. Open the application. Make the call. One step breaks the paralysis and lets momentum take over.

## Why "Should I Do It?" Deserves a Fast Answer

### The Cost of Indecision

Every hour you spend stuck is an hour you could have spent living. The question doesn't go away when you avoid it—it gets louder. The longer you wait, the more anxious you become, and the harder it gets.

A decision—even a wrong one—frees you. You learn from it. You adjust. You move forward. Indecision just keeps you stuck.

### The Paradox of Waiting

Here's something most people don't realize: waiting rarely makes the decision easier. You don't gain clarity by staying in limbo. You gain exhaustion. The decision feels harder because you're tired of it, not because you know more.

### Action Creates Information

When you act, you get data. When you don't act, you stay in the dark. A "wrong" decision teaches you something. Indecision teaches you nothing except that you're good at avoiding things.
