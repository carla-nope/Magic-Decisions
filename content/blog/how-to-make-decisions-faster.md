Picture this: You're standing in the cereal aisle for ten minutes, comparing labels like you're defusing a bomb. Sound familiar? Learning how to make decisions faster is a skill that can transform your daily life—from choosing what's for dinner to bigger choices that used to keep you up at night.

The good news? You don't need to become someone who doesn't care about outcomes. You just need to learn how to care about them *smarter*.

## Why We Overthink Simple Choices

Here's the thing about overthinking: it doesn't actually lead to better decisions. Research shows that excessive deliberation often makes us less confident in our choices and can even lead to decision paralysis. When you're a parent managing a household, every unnecessary mental minute spent deliberating is a minute stolen from something that actually matters.

The real problem isn't that we take decisions seriously. It's that we've forgotten how to distinguish between decisions that deserve deep thought and ones that just need to get made.

## The 70% Rule: When Good Enough Really Is Enough

Here's a mindset shift that changed everything for me: aim for 70% of the information rather than 100%. When you try to gather every scrap of data, compare every option exhaustively, and anticipate every possible outcome, you're not being thorough—you're stalling.

Ask yourself: "Do I have enough information to make a reasonable choice right now?" If yes, go with your gut. You can always course-correct later, but you can't get back the hours you spent deliberating.

## The Two-Category Framework

One of the simplest ways to make faster decisions is to sort choices into two buckets before you even start:

**Reversible decisions** — These are choices where you can easily change course if needed. What to make for dinner. Which park to visit this weekend. What time to start homework. Go with your first instinct on these.

**Irreversible decisions** — These are choices that are hard or impossible to undo. Major financial commitments, significant schedule changes, important relationship conversations. Take more time with these, but still set a deadline.

Most of the decisions that keep us up at night fall into the reversible category. Remind yourself of that when you're spiraling.

## Decision Traps to Watch For

When you want to make decisions faster, watch out for these common traps:

**Analysis paralysis** — The more options you consider, the harder decision-making becomes. Limit yourself to three options max before you start comparing.

**Perfectionism** — There is no perfect choice, only the choice you make and then make work. Perfect is the enemy of done.

**Other people's opinions** — Seeking input is valuable, but deferring to everyone else's judgment means you're not actually making a decision at all. Trust your instincts.

**Future forecasting** — We can't predict outcomes, no matter how much we analyze. Focus on the information you have now, not imaginary scenarios.

## A Simple Daily Practice

Start small. Each day, make one minor decision faster than usual. Order something new on the menu without reading every description. Pick the movie in five minutes instead of twenty. Say yes or no to an invitation within the hour instead of "let me think about it."

These tiny practices build your decision-making muscle. Over time, you'll find that your brain gets better at quickly sorting relevant information from noise—and you'll feel more confident trusting yourself.

## When Stakes Feel High

Even for bigger decisions, you can move faster without being reckless:

1. **Set a time limit.** Give yourself 24 hours for major choices, a week for life-changing ones. Write it down so you can't cheat.

2. **Write the pros and cons in two minutes flat.** Don't overthink it—just write fast. Your first instincts are often your truest ones.

3. **Ask yourself: "What would I tell my best friend to do?"** Distance from the problem often brings clarity.

4. **Visualize each option's outcome, not the decision itself.** How do you feel a month after choosing option A versus option B?

## The Bottom Line

You already know how to make decisions. You've been doing it your whole life. The only difference between now and tomorrow is practice—making choices, trusting yourself, and adjusting when needed.

Every decision you make, even the small ones, builds your confidence for the bigger ones. So start today. Pick something. Move forward. You can always course-correct, but you can't move forward while standing still.
