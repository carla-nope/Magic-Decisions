# When You Can't Decide, Use This Trick

You've been staring at the menu for five minutes. Your friend is done ordering. The waiter is waiting. And you still don't know if you want the pasta or the fish.

Sound familiar?

Decision paralysis doesn't just happen with big life choices. It happens with restaurants, Netflix shows, grocery items, weekend plans, and a hundred other tiny decisions that shouldn't matter but somehow do.

When you can't decide, here's a trick that works every time: **externalize the choice.**

## What Is the Externalization Trick?

The externalization trick is simple: when you're stuck, stop trying to decide internally and introduce an external element to make the choice for you.

Instead of asking "what do I want?" you ask "what does the coin say?" Instead of asking "should I or shouldn't I?" you ask "what does the oracle think?" Instead of agonizing over options, you let something outside your brain make the call.

The magic is in what this does to your brain. When you make a choice yourself, you own it. If it goes badly, you chose wrong. But when an external element chooses, the responsibility splits. The coin chose, not me. If it goes badly, the coin was wrong.

This isn't about being indecisive or avoiding responsibility. It's about recognizing that your brain is bad at certain types of decisions and better off receiving an answer than generating one.

### Why It Works: The Psychology

Here's what's happening neurologically when you can't decide:

Your prefrontal cortex is trying to calculate outcomes. It wants to predict the future, weigh probabilities, and optimize for the best result. But for everyday decisions, this is overkill. The pasta vs. fish question doesn't have a "best" answer—there's no way to know which you'll enjoy more. So the analysis runs and runs, looking for data that isn't there.

The externalization trick bypasses this. It tells your brain: "Stop calculating. The calculation is done. Here is your answer." And your brain, relieved of the impossible task of predicting the future, accepts it and moves on.

It's the same reason people consult oracle cards, horoscopes, or coaches for big decisions. Not because the cards know the answer—but because getting an external answer stops the loop.

## The Simple Version: Coin Flip

The most basic externalization tool is a coin. Heads you do it, tails you don't.

Try it right now. Think of something you've been putting off because you can't decide. A yes or no question. Flip a coin. See how you feel about the result.

Did you feel relieved? Good.

Did you feel disappointed? Even better. That disappointment is information. You wanted one outcome. Now you know what you actually wanted. The coin didn't choose for you—it revealed your preference.

This is the real power of the coin flip trick: it's not just a randomizer. It's a preference detector.

### The Five-Second Variation

Once you've flipped, watch your reaction in the first five seconds. That's your gut telling you the truth.

If you think "oh thank god, it said yes"—you wanted yes.
If you think "okay, I guess that's fine"—you're neutral, so accept the result.
If you think "ugh, really?"—you wanted the other option.

That five-second reaction tells you more about what you want than an hour of analysis. Use it.

## The More Advanced Version: Weighted Randomization

Basic coin flips work for yes/no. But what about situations with more options? That's where weighted randomization comes in.

Let's say you're choosing between three restaurants. But you kind of want Italian more than Mexican, even though you're also considering Thai. A standard randomizer treats all three equally, which might not reflect your actual preferences.

A weighted approach lets you adjust the odds. Maybe Italian gets two slots in the randomizer, Thai gets one, and Mexican gets one. Italian is more likely, but not guaranteed. This respects both your preference and your openness to something different.

This is the approach used by sophisticated decision tools like spin wheels with custom segments. You set the odds, the wheel spins, and the result reflects your input without being entirely in your control.

## When to Use the Trick

The externalization trick works for almost any decision, but it's especially useful in these situations:

### Analysis Paralysis
You've been going in circles. You've heard all the arguments. You know everything there is to know about the choice, and you're still stuck. This is the perfect moment for externalization. Your brain has run out of useful data—time to introduce new data.

### Low-Stakes Decisions
Not every decision deserves your full attention. What restaurant you go to tonight, what movie you watch, whether you go for a walk or stay in—these are low-stakes choices that your brain would prefer not to spend energy on. The trick saves that energy.

### Decision Fatigue
If you've been making decisions all day (and you probably have), your decision-making capacity is depleted. You don't have the mental resources for another choice. Externalization gives you an answer without spending your limited reserves.

### Group Decisions
When multiple people can't agree, externalization settles the debate. The coin or spinner doesn't play favorites. Everyone can accept "the tool chose" more easily than "someone else chose."

### When You're Looking for Permission
Sometimes you know what you want but you're afraid to claim it. You want to do the thing, but you're scared. You need permission, but there's no one to ask.

The trick gives you permission. "The oracle said yes, so I can do it." You wanted to do it anyway—the tool just gave you the green light you needed.

## The Exact Steps: How to Use This Trick Right Now

Here's what to do the next time you can't decide:

1. **State the decision clearly.** Write it down if needed. "Should I text them?" "Pasta or fish?" "Should I go to the gym?" The clearer, the better.

2. **Choose your tool.** Coin flip for yes/no. Spinner or randomizer for multiple options. Whatever tool you have handy.

3. **Set the stakes.** If it's multiple options, decide if you want equal weighting or weighted based on preference.

4. **Use the tool.** Flip, spin, generate. Get your result.

5. **Notice your reaction.** This is the most important step. How do you feel about the result? Relief? Disappointment? Neutrality? Your reaction tells you what you really wanted.

6. **Decide: accept or override.** If you're neutral or positive, accept and act. If you're strongly disappointed, override. The goal isn't to force yourself to do something you hate—it's to break the loop when you're genuinely stuck.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### Re-randomizing Immediately
You didn't like the result, so you flip again. And again. And again until you get the one you wanted.

This defeats the purpose. The trick works because you accept an external answer. If you keep re-randomizing until you get a specific result, you've just replaced internal overthinking with external overthinking.

Only re-randomize if the result is genuinely unacceptable, not just slightly disappointing.

### Using It for Big Decisions
The trick is for everyday indecision, not life-altering choices. You shouldn't use a coin flip to decide whether to quit your job or end a relationship. Those deserve real thought, even when that thought is hard.

### Forgetting the Five-Second Check
The reaction check is where the magic happens. If you skip it, you miss the information your gut is giving you. Always pause and notice how you feel.

## Building Decision Confidence

Here's something important: the goal isn't to rely on external tools forever. The goal is to use them as training wheels until your internal decision-making gets stronger.

Each time you use the trick, you're practicing a few things:
- Making decisions quickly
- Acting despite uncertainty
- Moving forward instead of staying stuck

Eventually, you'll find yourself making the call without the tool. The coin flip becomes internal. "I could flip a coin, but I already know what I want." That's the goal—not to always use the trick, but to internalize its function.

## When Nothing Else Works

Sometimes you genuinely can't decide even with external tools. You've flipped the coin, you've spun the wheel, and you still feel stuck.

This usually means one of two things:
1. The decision isn't yours to make right now. Walk away, sleep on it, come back tomorrow.
2. There's an underlying emotion blocking the decision. Fear, guilt, doubt. The tool can't fix those—only you can.

If you keep getting stuck on the same decision, stop using tools and start asking why. What are you afraid of? What would this choice mean about you? What would happen if you chose wrong?

Sometimes the real decision isn't about what to do—it's about whether you're ready to stop being stuck.

## The Takeaway

When you can't decide, the trick is to externalize the choice. Use a coin, a spinner, a randomizer, anything that takes the decision out of your hands and puts it somewhere else.

Why? Because your brain is bad at predicting the future and good at accepting answers. Give it an answer and watch it relax. Notice your reaction—that's the real data. Then decide whether to accept or override.

The goal isn't to let a coin make your choices. The goal is to stop the loop, get moving, and build the confidence to decide faster over time.
