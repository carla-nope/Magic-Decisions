Your 8-year-old has been staring at two t-shirts for five minutes. It's just a shirt. But for them, it feels like the biggest decision of their life.


    Sound familiar? You're not alone. Many kids get stuck on tiny decisions, turning what should take seconds into what feels like a high-stakes moment. The good news: this is totally fixable with some simple tools and language.


## Why Kids Overthink (It's Not What You Think)

*Learn more: [American Psychological Association on child resilience](https://www.apa.org/topics/child-development/resilience-infographic)*


    Adults often assume kids overthink because they're being dramatic, seeking attention, or testing boundaries. Sometimes that's true. But most of the time, here's what's actually happening:


### They're Still Learning How Decisions Work

    For adults, decision-making is automatic. We know that picking a shirt doesn't matter in the grand scheme. Kids haven't built that mental framework yet. To them, every choice can feel equally important.


### They Feel Pressure to Get It "Right"

    Kids often believe there's a perfect choice and a wrong choice. They don't yet know that for most things, any option works fine. This creates stress around decisions that adults find trivial.


### They're Mirroring Your Behavior

    If adults in the house visibly struggle with decisions or talk about "what if" scenarios, kids pick up on that energy. Overthinking becomes a family habit, not just a kid problem.


## The Simple Framework to Teach Them

    Here's a simple sentence you can teach your kids. Say it together until it becomes automatic:


    "Most decisions are small. Pick one and move on."


    Sounds too simple? It works. The goal isn't to make every decision feel meaningless. It's to help kids build a mental radar that says: "This is not a big deal decision. I can decide fast."


## The "Good Enough" Rule

    Teach kids about the concept of "good enough." Not every choice needs to be the perfect choice. A shirt that's not their absolute favorite still keeps them warm. A lunch that's not their top pick still fills their belly. A show that's not their favorite is still entertainment.


    Perfect is the enemy of done. This is a lesson that serves kids their whole lives. Starting with small decisions makes it easier to grasp.


## Use Tools to Remove the Pressure

    When kids struggle with making a choice, a tool can help. Not because they can't decide, but because tools remove the responsibility of being "right."


    Try these:

- **Coin flips:** For two options, just flip. Heads one, tails the other. Whatever lands is the choice. No stress, no second-guessing.
- **Spin wheels:** Put options on a wheel, spin, and done. Great for multiple choices like "which game to play" or "what order to do things in."
- **Yes/no tools:** For simple either/or decisions, sometimes you just need a clear answer. A quick digital coin or random picker removes all the drama.

## The Language That Helps

    Sometimes what helps kids isn't a tool — it's what you say. Try these phrases:


### "Pick one. You can change next time."

    This removes permanence. They know it's not forever, so the stakes feel lower.


### "There is no wrong choice here."

    Say this when you see them getting stuck. It's permission to stop analyzing.


### "What's the worst that could happen?"

    Usually the answer is "nothing." Sometimes kids just need to hear themselves say it.


### "Thirty seconds. Let's decide and move on."

    Give them a time limit. Challenge them. Make it a game. Most of the time, they can decide faster than they think.


## When Overthinking Signals Something Bigger

    Sometimes kids overthink because there's genuine anxiety underneath. If your child:

- Freezes completely on decisions that should be easy
- Can't sleep because they're replaying choices they made
- Constantly asks for reassurance after deciding
- Gets physically upset (crying, stomachaches, panic) over choices

    That's a signal to dig deeper. It might be anxiety, perfectionism, or something else worth addressing with more support. The tools and phrases above can still help, but they might need more context and patience.


## Practice Makes Progress

    Don't expect overnight change. Teaching kids to make faster decisions is a skill that develops over time. Start with low-stakes decisions (which cup, which snack, which toy) and work up from there.


    Celebrate when they decide quickly. Point it out: "You picked your outfit fast today! That showed good decision-making." Positive feedback builds the habit.


    And remember: you're teaching them a life skill. Years from now, they won't remember which shirt they picked today. But they'll remember that they learned to make decisions without getting stuck. That's the real lesson.
