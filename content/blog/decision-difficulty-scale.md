## The Decision Difficulty Scale: Know Exactly When to Think Hard (And When to Let Go)

*Learn more: [Pew Research: Social Media Use by Generation](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/fact-sheet/social-media/)*


Should you spend twenty minutes choosing a restaurant or two seconds flipping a coin? The answer depends on one thing most people never consider—the actual difficulty of the decision.

Imagine you're trying to pick a movie for family night. The options are: a Pixar animated film everyone has seen, or another Pixar animated film everyone has seen. You agonize for fifteen minutes. You read reviews. You check what's available on streaming. And ultimately, you pick something arbitrary because, honestly, it doesn't matter.

Now imagine you're choosing a school for your child. You gather information, visit campuses, talk to other parents, weigh options carefully. This decision deserves time and thought.

Both are "decisions." But one requires analysis, and one requires just picking something. The problem is that most families treat all decisions the same—they overthink the small stuff and underthink the big stuff. The solution? A simple decision difficulty scale that teaches your whole family to invest mental energy wisely.

## Understanding the Decision Difficulty Scale

The decision difficulty scale gives every choice a rating from 1 to 5. Once your family learns to use it, arguments decrease, decision fatigue lifts, and everyone starts making better choices faster. Here's how it works:
1
#### Trivial (Flip a Coin)

Would flipping a coin work perfectly? These are trivial decisions. What to eat for snack, which TV show to start with, whose turn to pick the music. Don't waste brain energy here.
2
#### Low Stakes (Quick Gut Decision)

Your gut knows this one. Which playground to visit, what color cup to use, which book to read at bedtime. Trust yourself and move on.
3
#### Medium Stakes (Light Deliberation)

Take a minute or two. Ask for input. Maybe look at a few options. Birthday gift ideas, weekend activity plans, which sporting event to attend. Worth some thought but don't spiral.
4
#### High Stakes (Serious Consideration)

Big purchases, major schedule changes, decisions affecting others' wellbeing. Take time. Gather information. Sleep on it if needed. But set a deadline to decide.
5
#### Life-Changing (Deep Analysis Required)

Career changes, major life moves, big financial decisions. These deserve full attention, possibly professional advice, and definitely careful weighing of values and priorities.

## Why Most Families Get This Wrong

Here's the pattern we see constantly: families spend enormous energy on level 1 decisions (which restaurant? what to watch?) and then panic their way through level 4 and 5 decisions without enough preparation. The overthinking happens in exactly the wrong places.

Why? Because small decisions feel urgent. Your kid is standing there waiting. Your spouse is asking the question right now. Meanwhile, the big decisions sit in the background, quietly neglected until they become emergencies.

### The Mental Energy Miscalculation

Your brain doesn't naturally distinguish between decision difficulty levels. It responds to the immediate pressure in front of it. That's why a whiny child asking "what are we doing today?" can hijack your entire day, while important but non-urgent decisions get forgotten.

The scale helps because it externalizes the process. Instead of your brain deciding how much energy to spend (badly), you consult a simple framework (well). It's like having a coach that says "slow down here, speed up there."

## Teaching Your Kids to Use the Scale

Kids as young as 5 or 6 can learn this. Start simple. At breakfast, ask: "Is this a flip-a-coin decision or a think-about-it decision?" Let them categorize choices with you. You'll be surprised how quickly they get it.

When they learn to say "that's a level 1, we don't need to argue about it," sibling disputes transform. Instead of fighting over who picks the game, they recognize the decision doesn't deserve their energy. They flip a coin or pick randomly and move on without resentment.

### Making It a Family Game

Turn the scale into a fun exercise. At family meetings, go through upcoming decisions and rate them together. Kids love putting decisions into categories. It makes them feel important and gives them language to express when something feels too big or too small to argue about.
> "We spend hours debating what to eat for dinner but minutes choosing a summer vacation. The scale helps us get this backward picture right."

## When to Use Tools and When to Decide Alone

Not every decision needs a tool. But for level 1 and 2 decisions, tools like spin wheels and coin flippers do something important: they make randomness feel fair. When two kids both want the same thing, a coin flip isn't a parent making a choice—it's the universe deciding. This removes parent from the equation and reduces "but that's not fair!" arguments.

For level 3 decisions, tools can still help when family members have equal say and equal stake. The key is recognizing that the goal isn't the perfect choice—it's moving forward without exhaustion.

## What Changes When You Use the Scale

Families who adopt the decision difficulty scale report remarkable changes within weeks. Dinner conversations become smoother because "what do you want to eat?" becomes "level 1 decision—random choice tonight!" Morning routines stop stalling because kids learn to make clothing choices in seconds instead of minutes.

More importantly, big decisions get the attention they deserve. When you stop wasting mental energy on where to go for a walk, you have more capacity for thinking through the actually important choices. Your brain stops being everywhere and starts being present where it matters.

## Start Using the Scale Today

Tonight at dinner, try this: go around the table and rate the upcoming weekend's main decision on the scale. Maybe it's a family activity. Maybe it's meal planning. Just practice categorizing and see how differently your family approaches the choice when everyone knows the difficulty level.
