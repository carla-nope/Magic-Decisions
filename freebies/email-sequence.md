## Email 3 — Day 5
**Subject:** the "I don't know" problem (and a simple fix)
**Trigger:** 5 days after Email 1

---

Hey {{first_name}},

Quick question: how many times did you ask your kid "what do you want for dinner?" this week?

And how many times did they answer "I don't know"?

Yeah. I thought so.

The "I don't know" loop is one of the most exhausting parts of parenting. The guide helps *you* stop overthinking — but what about when your kid is the one stuck?

Here's a simple approach that works surprisingly well:

**Instead of "what do you want?" — give them two options.**

Not ten options. Not an open-ended question. Two options, A or B.

"Cereal or toast?"

"Park or backyard?"

"This show or that show?"

It sounds almost too simple. And it is — which is exactly why it works. Kids (and adults, honestly) often don't have a strong preference. They're not being difficult. They just... don't know. Giving them a binary choice removes the pressure of having to generate an answer from scratch.

The guide has a whole category on family & kids decisions, but this approach works even outside those specific examples. You can adapt it to almost any situation where someone's decision-making is blocked.

**If you want to make it more fun:**

Use the spin wheel. Put the two options on the wheel, let them spin it, and whatever it lands on — that's what you do. They still "chose" it. You just made the choosing feel like a game.
