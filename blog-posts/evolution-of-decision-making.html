The Evolution of Decision-Making: How Growing Up Today Differs from 50 Years Ago

Your grandparents made decisions differently than you do. Your kids make them differently still. Understanding how decision-making has changed—and why—helps us raise children who thrive in the modern world.

Let's play a quick game. Imagine your 8-year-old self, circa whenever you grew up. What decisions did you make on a typical day? Now imagine a typical 8-year-old today. What decisions do they face?

If you're like most people, the second list is longer and more complex. The childhood decision landscape has changed dramatically in just one or two generations, and our children are navigating terrain we're not fully prepared to guide them through.

This isn't about nostalgia or saying "kids these days have it harder." It's about understanding the landscape so we can better equip our children to make decisions in the world they actually live in.

The Decision Landscape: Then vs. Now

The 1970s-1980s Childhood

Your Parents' Generation

Decision environment: Limited options, limited information, limited speed. When your parents were kids, choosing a cereal meant picking from 5 brands at the store, not 50 with online reviews. Picking a movie meant choosing from what was playing, not streaming thousands. Making plans meant calling friends on a landline, not coordinating 5 different schedules via group text.

  • Decision pace: Slow. You had days to decide, sometimes weeks.
  • Decision reversibility: High. Most choices could be undone.
  • Information access: Limited. You mostly learned from parents, teachers, and a few media sources.
  • Social comparison: Local. You compared yourself to kids in your school or neighborhood.

The 1990s-2000s Childhood

Your Generation

Decision environment: Expanding options, emerging internet, increasing pace. You grew up during the transition—part analog childhood, part digital awakening. You remember life before smartphones but grew up with them. Your decision-making skills developed during a period of rapid change.

  • Decision pace: Medium. You still had time to think, but options were increasing.
  • Decision reversibility: Still high for most choices.
  • Information access: Growing. Google appeared when you were in school. Suddenly answers were everywhere.
  • Social comparison: Regional. Television showed you how other kids lived, but it wasn't personalized.

The 2010s-2020s Childhood

Your Children's Generation

Decision environment: Infinite options, infinite information, infinite speed. Your kids face decisions that would have been impossible a generation ago. Should I post this? Will this follow me forever? What do I say to that comment? Who do I trust in this sea of information?

  • Decision pace: Often instant. Social media demands immediate responses.
  • Decision reversibility: Uncertain. Screenshots exist. The internet never forgets.
  • Information access: Overwhelming. Anyone can publish anything. Truth is hard to find.
  • Social comparison: Global and curated. Kids compare themselves to filtered highlight reels from around the world.

What This Means for Decision Skills

The changing landscape has created new challenges for decision-making:

Analysis Paralysis is Easier Than Ever

When you had 5 cereal options, you picked one in 30 seconds. When you have 500 cereal options online with reviews, ratings, ingredient lists, and comparisons, paralysis is rational. Your kids face this everywhere: what to watch, what to play, what to buy, what to believe. The abundance of options makes decision-making harder, not easier.

Permanence Makes Decisions Scarier

A wrong choice in 1985 was usually fixable. You changed schools, moved on, nobody knew. A wrong choice in 2025 might be screenshot, shared, archived, and resurfaced in job interviews. Kids know this. It makes them more hesitant to choose, more afraid of consequences. The stakes of decisions feel higher even when they're not.

Social Pressure is Constant and Global

When your decisions only affected your neighborhood, social pressure was manageable. Now kids face social pressure from their entire peer network (and beyond) 24/7. Every decision is visible: what you wear, what you say, what you like, what you don't like. The pressure to conform—or the pressure to stand out if you're countercultural—is exhausting.

"The children growing up today are the first generation to have their entire decision history potentially documented and accessible. This changes everything about how they approach choices."

The Skills Kids Need Today

Given these changes, the decision skills kids need today are different from what you needed:

Speed Over Perfection

In an environment of infinite options and instant response, the ability to decide quickly is more valuable than ever. Perfect is the enemy of good, and in the modern world, "good enough and moving forward" beats "perfect and stuck." Kids need to learn: make a choice, commit to it, adjust if needed.

Resilience Over Avoidance

Because decisions feel higher-stakes, kids are tempted to avoid them. "Whatever you want" becomes a survival strategy. But avoidance doesn't build decision confidence—it builds decision anxiety. Kids need to practice deciding even when it's scary, and they need to learn that bad outcomes are survivable and learnable from.

Filtering Over Consuming

In an information-saturated world, the ability to filter is essential. Kids need to learn: not everything is worth evaluating, not every option deserves consideration, sometimes less information leads to better decisions. The skill isn't knowing more—it's knowing what to ignore.

Identity Over Imitation

With global comparison at their fingertips, kids are tempted to build their identity based on what they see others doing. But authentic decision-making comes from knowing yourself—what you value, what you want, what you stand for. Kids need practice making decisions that align with their own values rather than just following the crowd or rebelling from it.

How Parents Can Help

Given the changed landscape, here are specific ways to help your kids develop modern decision skills:

Teach "Good Enough" Decision-Making

Explicitly teach that perfection isn't the goal. Share your own examples: "I spent 30 minutes choosing a restaurant last week, and honestly, it wasn't worth it. The food was fine. The extra time was wasted." Help them see that decision quality and decision speed can both be high.

Practice Reversibility

When decisions turn out badly, help them see the reversibility: "That shirt didn't work out? Return it. The haircut grew out. The friendship can be rebuilt. Most decisions can be adjusted." This reduces the paralysis that comes from fear of permanent consequences.

Create Decision-Free Zones

Give them space from the constant decision pressure of modern life. Screen-free times, simplified options at home, rotating choices that don't require their input. Their decision muscles need rest as well as exercise.

Celebrate Decisions, Not Just Outcomes

"You made a choice and owned it" should be celebrated as much as "you made the right choice." This builds the identity of being a decider, not just someone who gets lucky sometimes.

Use Tools to Build Confidence

Spin wheels, coin flips, and random pickers are training wheels for the modern decision environment. They teach: decisions get made, outcomes happen, and the world continues. This confidence transfers to higher-stakes situations when they're ready.

Looking Forward

We don't know what decision environment your children will face as adults. AI will change things. New technologies will create new challenges. But the fundamental skills—deciding, committing, adjusting, learning—will remain valuable.

The goal isn't to prepare your kids for the world as it is. It's to prepare them to thrive in the world as it will be. And that world will require confident, resilient decision-makers who can navigate complexity without freezing.

You're equipping your children with skills that will serve them for their entire lives. Every time you help them practice deciding, you're building capacity they'll use in contexts we can't even imagine yet. That's powerful work.
